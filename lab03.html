Visualization Critique

Visualization

Critique

Information Resolution

While the infographic is visually striking, it falls short of what Edward Tufte describes as high information resolution. Tufte argues that a graphic’s strength lies in its ability to present a high density of data, allowing the viewer to make comparisons and identify patterns within a single glance. This visualization, however, prioritizes "beautiful" minimalism over technical depth. The vast purple area is essentially "chartjunk". Which is an empty space that conveys very little actual data. Because the x-axis only labels three specific years and the y-axis lacks a visible scale or gridlines, the viewer is unable to determine the values for the fluctuations between 2007 and 2019 and makes it very confusing. The resolution is further lowered by the use of "million tonnes of oil equivalent," a technical conversion that abstracts the physical reality of coal burning into a secondary metric, making it harder for a general audience to grasp the tangible impact on the environment.

Effects wihtout Causes

The graphic also suffers from presenting effects without causes. The dramatic plunge in coal consumption after 2019 is presented as a miraculous, self-evident trend, yet the visualization provides no context for the massive structural shifts that produced this outcome. In reality, this decline was the result of the decommissioning of Portugal’s two largest coal-fired power plants, Pego and Sines, alongside a significant increase in EU carbon pricing. By stripping away these causal factors, the graphic suggests an inevitable decline rather than a complex transition fueled by specific policy decisions and investments in renewable infrastructure. It leaves the user with the "what" but completely ignores the "how," turning a complex industrial transition into a simple aesthetic slope.

Cherry Picking

Furthermore, there is a distinct element of cherry-picking regarding the timeline and the global context of the data. By ending the primary data set in 2021, the graphic captures a period heavily influenced by the COVID-19 pandemic, which saw a global suppression of industrial energy demand. Presenting this sharp drop without acknowledging the temporary downward pressure of the pandemic could lead a viewer to believe the transition was smoother or faster than the long-term structural reality might suggest. Additionally, the visualization blurs the line between historical data and "projected" outcomes. By merging the two into a single, continuous shaded shape, it risks overreaching, lending the same level of certainty to future forecasts as it does to verified historical facts.

Conclusion

Overall, this graphic is reaches it's aesthetic goal of modern minalism, but the lacks analytical impact. It shows that coal is down, but strips away the resolution needed to understand the complexity of Portugal's energy grid.
